I'm Peter Silverstein, a current master's student pursuing dual degrees in Quantitative Methods for the Social Sciences and Public Administration at Columbia University. At my core, I am an urbanist and someone who strongly believes in the importance of cities in the future of sustainability. I combine my single-minded focus on urban policy with a robust set of data analysis techniques, all of which allow me to explore the rich and intricate patterns in urban environments with precision and creativity.

I started my career in marketing, which stemmed from a passion for understanding how and why consumers behave in the ways that they do. In that previous life, I conducted academic research on large, corporate retailers entering the secondhand space, a burgeoning trend that has exploded in relevance over the past five years. Professionally, I was a research analyst performing advertising effectiveness research at Dynata, the world’s largest single-source panel provider. At Dynata, I directly managed brand lift research for several worldwide brands and was directly responsible for around $1.5 million in revenue per year. In this role, I not only developed understanding of key survey research methods (e.g., inference testing, survey/question design, weighting, crosstabs), but I became a well-rounded research manager with experience in project tracking, research consultation, insight writing, and reporting. Although my interests have diverged from the advertising industry, these sorts of research skills are relevant to any social science researcher, regardless of subject area.

I oriented myself towards sustainable urban policy because of my passion for sustainability and my love for cities. Cities are a mosaic of chaotic and yet predictable behaviors—the ultimate expression of humanity and a rewarding subject to ask research questions about. With this central pillar in mind, I have begun to build a diverse set of data analysis and research tools that will afford me flexibility and creativity in my investigations. In addition to a robust understanding of the technical basics of data analysis (hypothesis testing, regression and predictive modeling, data wrangling and manipulation), I employ spatial analysis via GIS and mapping to be able to characterize relationships in terms of space, a key aspect of city life. As my education is progressing, I am adding skills in Bayesian multilevel modeling, data visualization, and machine learning. The confluence of all these methods, alongside the second part of my dual degree: a master's in public administration, will allow me to be a dynamic researcher with the ability to speak and connect to a wide range of people, all of whom have interesting things to say about city policy.

To me, the key to my ability to make positive impact in sustainable urban policy is that dynamism. My career goal is not to be a data scientist or policy analyst, but instead to become a leader in the space with the capacity to contribute to and understand quantitative approaches to social research. My line of thinking is that too much good quantitative research is diminished due to lack of understanding by a general audience, and that lots of great policy ideas don't receive the rigorous quantitative analysis they need to become effective policy. I am someone who will bridge that gap and contribute to the progress towards sustainable, equitable, and prosperous cities.

Personally, I am an avid user of public transportation, buyer of used clothes and books, walker of longish and shortish walks, and baker of English muffins. Although I am a registered Democrat, I have a small collection of antique Republican president memorabilia.
